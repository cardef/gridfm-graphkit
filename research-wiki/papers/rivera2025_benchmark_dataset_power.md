# PF$Δ$: A Benchmark Dataset for Power Flow under Load, Generation, and Topology Variations

## One-line thesis
PFΔ, an 859,800-sample power-flow benchmark spanning load, generator-cost, and N-1/N-2 topology perturbations plus close-to-infeasible cases, exposes generalization gaps in SOTA GNN solvers.

## Problem / Gap
Existing GNN-for-power-flow papers each train/evaluate on custom, non-standardized data covering only a subset of real-world variability — e.g., TypedGNN and GraphNeuralSolver omit N-1 perturbations, PowerFlowNet is untested on N-1/N-2, and CANOS is not trained on a realistically diverse set of load/generator profiles — making cross-paper comparison meaningless. ACOPF-oriented datasets that do span N-1/N-2 or large grids (OPFData, OPF-Learn) are unsuited to PF benchmarking because ACOPF setpoints are near-cost-optimal and thus lack the generator-setpoint diversity PF must handle.

## Method
Each sample is generated by simultaneously (1) sampling loads from the OPF-Learn convex-feasible-region method, (2) applying one of four equally probable N-0/N-1/N-2 outage events (remove ≤2 generators, ≤2 lines, one line + one generator, or nothing — the OPFData perturbation scheme), and (3) randomly permuting generator cost-function parameters to diversify active-power/voltage setpoints — then solving a custom ACOPF formulation (PowerModels.jl + Ipopt) with output-variable limits (reactive-power generation, PQ-bus voltage magnitude, branch flow) removed so the ACOPF constraints reduce exactly to the PF equations; only converged samples are kept. Close-to-infeasible cases are produced separately via MATPOWER's continuation power flow — a predictor-corrector trace of a randomly chosen load/generator injection pair scaled toward 2.5× its base value — up to the "NOSE" bifurcation point where the PF Jacobian becomes singular, saving the four samples immediately preceding it as "approaching-infeasible" augmentation data. Three GNN baselines are adapted to this setting: CANOS-PF (an interaction-network encoder-decoder, re-implemented from CANOS with sigmoid output bounding removed, its constraint-violation loss narrowed to power/branch-flow violations, and slack-bus values now predicted rather than fixed), PowerFlowNet (bus-typed + mask embeddings with K-localized TAGConv message passing, L2 loss), and GNS-S (a modified GraphNeuralSolver that self-supervises by directly minimizing power-balance mismatch, with PV generators fixed at their prescribed output so only the slack bus is adjusted).

## Key Results
- Models trained only on the base topology (N) generalize poorly to N-1/N-2; training on N-1 improves generalization to both N-1 and N-2 across all three GNNs, but none reach Newton-Raphson-level power-balance loss even with physics-informed components (Task Group 1).
- OOD size transfer (train on 118-bus, test on 57-/500-/2000-bus, Task 3.1): GNN models are up to ~5× faster than Newton-Raphson but never reach NR's ~1e-6 power-balance precision; NR itself converges from a flat start only 95.2%/65.7%/43.4% of the time on the 57-/118-/500-bus systems, and not at all on the 2000-bus system.
- On close-to-infeasible (C2I) cases, PFNet performs worst overall; training only on hard/C2I samples (Task 4.3) improves C2I accuracy but sharply degrades PFNet's feasible-case accuracy, whereas CANOS-PF and GNS-S retain strong feasible-case accuracy even in that regime, attributed to their physics-informed components.

## Assumptions
- Grids are drawn from six pglib-derived benchmark cases (IEEE-14/30/57/118, GOC-500, GOC-2000); no real utility topology is used.
- A single PF solution per sample is accepted — the one Ipopt converges to from its default initialization — rather than enumerating the multiple solutions a nonlinear PF system can admit.
- Generator cost-function permutation is used purely to diversify active-power/voltage setpoints, not to model realistic economic dispatch.

## Limitations / Failure Modes
- GOC-2000 gets only 61,800 samples (vs. 159,600 for the other five grids) because scaling the data-generation pipeline to larger grids was computationally infeasible within the authors' compute budget.
- No tested model — including the physics-informed GNS-S and CANOS-PF — matches NR's power-balance precision (~1e-6) in any regime, and all three GNNs struggle to generalize across grid sizes (largest grid tested is ~2,000 buses; the paper explicitly flags scaling to >1000-bus, real-transmission-scale grids as unresolved).
- The benchmark has no mechanism for a model to detect/flag infeasible inputs (only near-infeasible ones), and contingencies are capped at N-2 — N-k for k>2 is left as future work given its combinatorial growth.

## Reusable Ingredients
- Continuation-power-flow-based close-to-infeasible case generation: use MATPOWER's continuation solver to scale a random load+generator injection pair toward the Jacobian-singularity ("NOSE") bifurcation, and save the last few pre-bifurcation samples as "approaching-infeasible" augmentation data — a general recipe for stress-testing any PF/OPF learner near voltage-stability limits.
- The "ACOPF with output limits removed" trick: dropping reactive-power, PQ-bus-voltage, and branch-flow bounds turns any existing ACOPF solver/data pipeline (PowerModels.jl + Ipopt) into a generic PF-instance generator.
- The 4-task-group standardized eval protocol (in-distribution topology generalization, data efficiency, OOD grid-size transfer, hard/C2I-case training) plus the unsupervised Power Balance Loss metric (mean/max |ΔS_i| from the PF mismatch equations) — usable to score any PF/OPF surrogate without depending on a solver-specific ground-truth solution.

## Open Questions
- Can an architecture close the gap to NR-level power-balance precision at OOD grid sizes without either the oversmoothing risk of deep GNNs or a novel size-generalization mechanism, given all three tested GNNs struggle at size transfer (Task Group 3)?
- Can a model learn to detect/flag infeasible power-flow inputs — as opposed to just approaching them — given that NR solvers give no diagnostic signal on non-convergence and no such benchmark currently exists?

## Abstract (original)

> Power flow (PF) calculations are the backbone of real-time grid operations, across workflows such as contingency analysis (where repeated PF evaluations assess grid security under outages) and topology optimization (which involves PF-based searches over combinatorially large action spaces). Running these calculations at operational timescales or across large evaluation spaces remains a major computational bottleneck. Additionally, growing uncertainty in power system operations from the integration of renewables and climate-induced extreme weather also calls for tools that can accurately and efficiently simulate a wide range of scenarios and operating conditions. Machine learning methods offer a potential speedup over traditional solvers, but their performance has not been systematically assessed on benchmarks that capture real-world variability. This paper introduces PF$Δ$, a benchmark dataset for power flow that captures diverse variations in load, generation, and topology. PF$Δ$ contains 859,800 solved power flow instances spanning six different bus system sizes, capturing three types of contingency scenarios (N , N -1, and N -2), and including close-to-infeasible cases near steady-state voltage stability limits. We evaluate traditional solvers and GNN-based methods, highlighting key areas where existing approaches struggle, and identifying open problems for future research. Our dataset is available at https://huggingface.co/datasets/pfdelta/pfdelta/tree/main and our code with data generation scripts and model implementations is at https://github.com/MOSSLab-MIT/pfdelta.
